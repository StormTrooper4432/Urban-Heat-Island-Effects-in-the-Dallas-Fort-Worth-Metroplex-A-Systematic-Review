Panel Q&A briefing notes

Use this page for likely technical and policy questions during presentation, with concise responses tied to your implemented pipeline.

What does the model do?

The system produces monthly UHI estimates across DFW and forecasts monthly aggregate UHI severity. It is designed for place-based heat-risk diagnosis and short-horizon planning support.

Why trust the data?

The pipeline uses established NASA, NOAA, ECMWF, USGS, and JRC products with documented QA metadata. Reliability statements and source links are presented in the methodology page.

Likely questions and direct answers

Q: Why is ML R2 not very high?
A: Leakage controls removed target-derived predictors. This yields realistic performance; the model still improves over seasonality-only baseline.
Q: What is the strongest result?
A: ARIMA materially outperforms seasonal naive on monthly forecasting, supporting operational scheduling use cases.
Q: Is this model only DFW?
A: Yes. Sampling and preprocessing both enforce DFW boundaries.
Q: What assumptions matter most?
A: Stable monthly seasonality, valid rural-mask baseline, and nonlinear predictor-response structure.
Q: What are key limitations?
A: Bounding-box geography, LST-based target vs direct air-temperature exposure, and residual heterogeneity at local scales.

Defensible claims

  • The workflow is reproducible and auditable from source to figures.
  • Leakage safeguards were explicitly implemented and verified.
  • Temporal forecasting utility is benchmarked, not asserted.
  • Feature relevance aligns with physical intuition (seasonality, urban intensity, vegetation).

Recommended next iterations

  • Replace bounding box with administrative polygons for finer governance relevance.
  • Add uncertainty quantification for cross-sectional ML predictions.
  • Integrate intervention scenario features (tree expansion, impervious reduction).
  • Add external validation with station-level air-temperature proxies.
